The Clear-Instruction Checklist (Shop Floor)

On a real floor, a misread instruction is not a typo; it is a defect, a delay, or an incident. Clarity is a safety and quality skill, not a nicety. This checklist makes instructions land under real conditions.

Write to be understood, not just issued

  • Use plain language operators actually use, not head-office phrasing.
  • Confirm back safety-critical steps; do not assume they landed.
  • Design for the conditions: noise, pace, pressure, literacy, language.

Back it up and check it

  1. Show, do not just tell, where a demonstration is clearer.
  2. Pass context at handover, not just the task.
  3. Make it safe to flag a confusing instruction.

Blame the instruction first

When a mistake happens, check whether the instruction was clear before blaming the operator. Treating misreads as a communication problem, not a discipline one, is how a floor gets safer and better over time.
